01The Case

The argument in one page

When the question becomes how much ground clean power actually needs, nuclear's compactness is a quiet but decisive advantage.

02How it works

Inside a nuclear power plant

No mystery and no green goo. A fission plant is, at heart, a steam engine running on an extraordinarily dense fuel.

  1. Fission heats water. Splitting uranium atoms in the reactor core releases heat, with no burning, no smoke, and no CO₂.
  2. Steam spins a turbine. That heat boils water into high-pressure steam, which drives the turbine blades.
  3. The generator makes electricity. The spinning shaft turns a generator, the same principle used in coal, gas, and hydro plants.
  4. Power flows to the grid. A transformer steps up the voltage and sends it down the transmission lines.
  5. The towers release water vapor. The famous white plume is not smoke and not radioactive. It is the same vapor as a boiling kettle.

Read that chart again. Coal is roughly 820 times deadlier than nuclear per unit of energy, yet it is nuclear that carries the public fear. The deaths caused by coal are invisible because they are diffuse: thousands of cases of respiratory and cardiovascular disease spread across a population, never attributed to a single dramatic event. Our perception of risk tracks visibility, not body count, and that single cognitive bias has shaped a generation of energy policy.

04Capacity

How often does each source actually produce power?

Capacity factor is the share of the year a plant spends generating at full power. Nuclear runs at roughly 92%, around the clock, in every season. No other major source comes close.

Nuclear All other sources

Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration.

Baseload sources

Nuclear, geothermal, coal, gas. Can run continuously, providing reliable power 24/7 regardless of weather.

Intermittent sources

Solar, wind, hydro (seasonal). Depend on the weather, so they need backup power or storage to fill the gaps.

Wind and solar are essential and growing, but they are variable. A grid built entirely on weather-dependent sources needs something to fill the gaps when the weather doesn't cooperate. That "firm" clean power is exactly what nuclear provides, and the alternative gap-filler, today, is almost always natural gas.

07Exposure

Radiation, in everyday context

You are exposed to radiation every day, from rocks, the sky, and your own food. The question is never "is there radiation?" but "how much?"

What is a millisievert?

A millisievert (mSv) measures radiation dose. The average person receives about 3 mSv per year from natural background radiation: cosmic rays, radon, and radioactive elements in soil and rock.

The key insight

Living beside a nuclear plant adds 0.01 mSv a year, about 1/300th of natural background. A single cross-country flight gives you four times more.

08Myths

Myths vs. facts

Common misconceptions about nuclear energy, corrected with evidence. Tap a myth to see what the data says.

FactThe entire 70-year history of U.S. commercial nuclear power has produced about 90,000 metric tons of used fuel, all of it solid, tracked, and stored safely on-site in dry casks. Stacked together, it fits within the area of a single football field. Unlike fossil-fuel waste, it never leaves human control.

FactYou receive more radiation from one cross-country flight (0.04 mSv) than from living next to a nuclear plant for an entire year (0.01 mSv), and both are a tiny fraction of the ~3 mSv everyone receives annually from natural background radiation.

FactA reactor physically cannot detonate like a nuclear weapon. The fuel is nowhere near enriched enough, and the physics are fundamentally different. Weapons require precise configurations that a power reactor cannot produce by accident.

FactA new generation of designs, including small modular reactors and molten-salt reactors, is engineered around one principle: walking away is the safety feature. They shut themselves down through the laws of physics, failing safe instead of failing loud. The fear that froze nuclear development in the 20th century is being engineered out of the 21st.

FactAdvanced reactors can reuse spent fuel, and thorium, an alternative fuel, is virtually inexhaustible. Between recycling and new fuel cycles, nuclear's fuel supply is measured in centuries, not decades.

Why do these myths persist? Decades of Cold War association, high-profile accidents taken out of context, and a public understanding of radiation shaped more by movies than by measurement. The data tells a different story.
